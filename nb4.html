Prospectus Summarization and Main Challenges

As stated in our prospectus, we originally set out to find the correlation between obesity and various socioeconomic factors such as education levels, population density, poverty levels, etc.

To do so, we realized that we would need a large amount of data to find strong enough correlations. As such, a state by state basis wouldn't cut it. We needed to go deeper and find data on a county level. As such, we gathered data from over 3000 US counties on each of the variables we chose to examine.

Additionally, given the size of the data, we were initially concerned with removing all discrepancies. For instance, among the 3000+ US counties we had data on, there were various NA or missing values within the lists. This was so because some counties were too small to have data recorded. So for examples, poverty levels were recorded in some counties and not others.

As a result, we had to be very meticulous in gathering all NA or missing value counties and eliminating them from the data analysis. While doing so brought down our total number of counties analyzed to 2000+, we feel that this was a better outcome for a few reasons. Primarily, it removed the small, insignificant counties that were far more likely to contain bizarre outliers as a result of their size. Second, 2000+ counties was still a very significant amount of data points to analyze considering the different number of variables we were looking at.

Finally, we were considered about the initial assumptions made in isolating some variables and reading into what our data tells us. While using regression can tell us that there is a relationship between, say, poverty and obesity, we also know that correlation does not equal causation.

When coming out with our results and summarizing them, we had to be careful not to assign definitive causation to any strong correlation we found. So while our results would certainly be more definitive and eye-popping if we could assert that X or Y caused obesity, we were careful to make sure not to fall into such traps.

Our Data Sources

Our data came primarily from www.data.gov the US government's database on information regarding US states, counties, and municipalities. As it's from an official government database, all data is both accurate, reliable, and up-to-date.

From this site we downloaded a number of files regarding health and other information on US counties. These files included and were called:

  1. Demographics: This file presented a demographic breakdown of the counties; from this file we took out "poverty rates", "population size", and "population density" as variables to be isolated and compared.

  2. Summary Measures of Health: This file presented various health statistics about each county; from this file we took out "average life expectancy", "health status", and "number of unhealthy days" out to be compared.

  3. Risk Factors and Access to Care: This file examined various risk factors among the population in each county along with their ability to access medical care; from this we took out "Uninsured Rate", "Number of Smokers", "Obesity Rate", and "Number of Primary Care Physicians per 100,000" to evaluate.

  4. Vulnerable Populations and Environmental Health: This file presented the number of "vulnerable" populations in the counties as well as the health of their surroundings (pollution); from this we took out "Rate of People w/o a High School Diploma", "Unemployment Rate", "Depression Rate", "People with Recent Drug Use".

That was a total of 14 different variables evaluated. Originally the data all came in XLS form which we converted to CSV. Finally, because all the data was so consolidated in one central location at www.data.gov, we actually found that gathering the data was rather easy. The more difficult part was simply choosing which variables to isolate.

What was surprising to us, however, was the lack of strong correlation between smokers (as we thought engaging in risky health behavior like smoking would increase obesity) and a lack of High School education with obesity.

Final Summary of the Data

All in all we find out a few things about the variables:

Positive Correlations (Ex: Higher the poverty rate, higher the obesity rate)

  1. Poverty: porverty rate per US county
  2. Health Status: rate of population who report "poor" health
  3. Unemployment Rate: unemployment rate per US county

Negative Correlations

  1. Population Size
  2. Population Density
  3. Average Life Expectancy
  4. Uninsured Rate
  5. Primary Care Physicians per 100,000 People
  6. Depression Rate
  7. Recent Drug Use Rate

With the correlations we see, this is the final conclusion we have come to. Rural areas have higher correlation with obesity than urban areas would (which is consistent with research we found online). Additionally we see that poverty levels and unemployment rates have positive correlations with obesity. We also see that uninsured rates and the number of primary care physicians per 100,000 people have negative correlations with obesity.

With all this in mind, we see that a lack of access (shown by primary care physicians, uninsurance rates) and an inability to utilize existing resources (shown by poverty, unemployment rates) have high correlations with obesity. While we will not outright make a claim that it causes obesity, our data shows that there are visible correlations between the factors.
